This novel explores individuality and personhood with greater depth than its predecessor, especially through Mahit’s evolving relationships with both Yskandr and Three Seagrass. The Aliens (the Enemy) and Twenty Cicada (Swarm) bring these questions to a natural and compelling conclusion.

The love story between Mahit and Reed (Three Seagrass) felt organic and unforced. The sex scene — the first lesbian sex scene I’ve read — was visceral and real without being pornographic. It felt human. Martine’s female perspective is unmistakable throughout; she writes female characters with precision, empathy, and emotional depth.

Eight Antidote is particularly well drawn. His youthful sense of morality plays beautifully against the palace’s layered, often cynical politics. In contrast, I didn’t feel Martine’s male characters were as fully realised. They often seemed to occupy the background and lacked a kind of male energy — a presence of movement, speech, or desire — that’s difficult to pin down. Their motivations and internal conflicts felt more implied than explored. This may have been a deliberate choice.

The Teixcalaanli world itself leans into the poetic, refined, and graceful — qualities that feel feminine in tone — even as it's underpinned by a martial, imperialist structure. That tension is fascinating. Six Direction, the previous emperor, was an exception: complex and well developed. So too was the imperial associate. And One Lightning’s arrogance and brute force could be read as a deliberate critique of a certain vision of maleness.

This emphasis makes sense from an author writing from a feminist — possibly queer-feminist — point of view. The pacing was excellent. The shifts between The Jewel of the World, Lsel Station, and the fleet kept the tension high and the pages turning. A colourful book, rich in intrigue and adventure. Thoroughly enjoyed.
