My favourite of the Malazan Book of the Fallen so far. The central theme in this novel is compassion — compassion for the self and for others. We see this quality embodied in Anomander Rake (“What he does he does for others”), in Nimander, Chaur, Gruntle, Monkrat, Bridgeburner Setoc in her hair shirt, and Harllo’s friend, Orfantal. Most poignantly, there is Pearl, carrying the burdens of others within the dark world of Dragnipur, Rake’s sword. For these characters, compassion and selflessness are as natural as breathing.

Other figures reveal compassion in conflict — at war with pride, vengeance, or despair — yet capable of redemption. Kallor, the “High King,” shows compassion even as he kills Endest Silann, Rake’s loyal Tiste Andii lieutenant. He also articulates the book’s moral centre: “Compassion without action is mere sentimentality, self-indulgence masquerading as virtue.”

The broader themes of the series deepen here, and Erikson’s critique of organized religion grows sharper through metaphor — the Dying God’s blood, Saemankelyk, echoing Christian communion, and the Crippled God’s faith promising only death after a life of suffering — a reflection on both Christianity and Capitalism that flows seamlessly into the figure of the Redeemer, a quiet, Christ-like symbol of uncorrupted compassion. Yet the figure of the Redeemer remains quietly Christ-like: a good and gentle god before institutional power inevitably corrupts, as Seerdomin comes to understand.

The pacing and structure are classic Erikson — long, immersive chapters of reflection and world-building that accelerate into brisk, interwoven climaxes.

Mysteries are revealed and new ones arise. Traveller is unveiled as Dassem Ultor, the First Sword of the Malazan Empire, while Kruppe’s true nature lingers tantalisingly beyond certainty — immortal, god, or simply a man whose wisdom transcends mortality.

By the end, I felt both exhausted and exhilarated, reminded that compassion — the heart of this novel — endures as the quiet thread binding all its stories together. Toll the Hounds gathers everything Erikson has been building — the moral inquiry, the compassion, the laughter in the face of despair — and weaves it into art. It made me reconsider what this series is really about. Not war, gods, or empires, but ordinary kindness and the cost of carrying others' pain. If the earlier books tested my patience and faith in the story, this one rewarded it completely. It left me humbled, and deeply grateful to be reading the Malazan Book of the Fallen.
