Finished: 2025-11-17

> A wild space adventure with little emotional depth.

The novel was very much a middle chapter. The plot for the conclusion is set-up with the monsters in the mist revealed, the dark and conflicted motivations and actions of the Marble Fleet (Fleet of Knives) explored.

I liked that no character is safe with Powell. Alva Clays death was brutal and quick. Overwhelmed by a terrible force of alien parasite monsters. Powell drew the shock and trauma of the death well with the grief that attached Sal felt real and drawn. Though the death did not move me. I found myself interested in how Sal would react and this would impact the story. While this may sound callous Powell had not softened and rounded the character enough for me to feel the emotional punch. He attempts to soften Alva when Sal asks if she will be a sister to Trouble Dog and herself. But the character was not sufficiently developed. She is a hulking and moody presence on the crew.  The surviving crew (and of course Lucy) of Lucy's Ghost were ready placed to be replacements.

In my view the multi-view-point structure worked. But Powell lost an opportunity here. The internal turmoil of the characters and the Trouble Dog's role of observer could have been used to add greater emotional depth. Indeed missunderstandings between the crew could have been drawn to create dramatic irony helping the reader become more engaged with the characters.

Powell's characters interact in a transactional manner and the action takes precedence. In contrast to the Expanse by Correy which is similarly action paced the characters feel one dimensional. In the Expanse the loss and trauma and emotional impact on the reader are more pronounced. The only difference I can draw is the space for the characters to grow in the readers mind.

The key tropes of a space opera are there. Alien artifact (the "Restless Itch"), inscrutable and hostile aliens and an ensemble cast. The presence of these tropes feels more contrived then in the Expanse of Hamilton's Commonwealth Saga.

Ona Sudak's ark is drawn. She is doing terrible things for an apparent noble cause. A path she has walked before. I would not be surprised if she has a redemption in the third book. I find myself interested in what shape this will take and if Powell will surprise us.

I'm not sure, yet, that there is a clear moral theme to the story. There is a discussion of grief (Sal, Trouble Dog, Alva) regret (Sal, Trouble Dog) and the lost opportunity for redemption (Ona) but I felt these were incidental rather than central. Is Powell painting by numbers or is he going to be able to bring these themes into clear focus in his final installment?

In summary I found the novel formulaic with little emotional resonance. But very enjoyable to read. Who doesn't love a space adventure? I great read for the beach.
