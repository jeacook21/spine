>A glittering tragedy of love and illusion — dazzling to the eye, but cold to the touch.

Evocative and beautifully written. Fitzgerald draws a sketch of the Gilded Age in the US where life, money, and love were cheap. The novel is a satire of a way of life, a way of thinking and behaving that is timeless — the worship of money and status, the hypocrisy of “old” money disdaining the new, and the way those with power treat those without.

I found it hard to love any of the characters. Gatsby’s love of Daisy, whose “voice is full of money,” is not pure, at least in my reading. He loves her for her status — for all she has and he has not, for where she comes from and he does not. His hard work, his building of wealth, and his achievements in the war are all to obtain what he can never be. It is up to the reader to interpret this cynically or as a tragedy. Both Daisy and Gatsby are ultimately unhappy. Is this due to societal constraint or their own vanity?

If Gatsby’s obsession reveals the tragedy of idealised love, Tom represents the brutal opposite: a man defined by entitlement and power. Tom is a caricature of a toxic masculine bore — characteristically selfish, self-justifying, and cruel. His love for his wife is transactional; Daisy is not sure why she loves him, and Tom loves Daisy for the respectable veneer she provides.

Nick, the narrator, is both judgmental and torn — enthralled by the glamorous world he brushes against but repulsed by it. His enchantment and revulsion are key to the novel’s ironic voice. In the end, he loves Gatsby (I think), yet is repulsed by how he made his money and even more so by the hypocrisy and coldness of the world of West Egg and New York.

The shallowness of the social world Gatsby inherits is drawn beautifully — the abandonment of Gatsby when the champagne has stopped flowing and the party has ended, with only a few people attending his funeral. If there is a parable here, it is that wealth does not create happiness or love, only loneliness while being surrounded by people who care nothing for you, only for what you can give them.

While the world is beautifully drawn and the characterisation vivid, I struggled through much of the book. I found it hard to empathise with the characters and felt the coldness of that world keenly. As such, I struggled to enjoy the novel; I am left wondering if Fitzgerald intended it to be enjoyed or merely _witnessed_. His prose is dazzling even as it exposes the emptiness beneath the glamour. As a work of art, it deserves its place as a classic of American and global literature.
