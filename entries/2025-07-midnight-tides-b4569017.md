I was initially disappointed by the absence of the Bridgeburners and other familiar faces — Erikson's abrupt shift to an entirely new continent and cast felt like a gamble. But by the end, I found myself deeply moved and impressed. Midnight Tides introduced a fresh, richly drawn group of characters, and among them, Trull Sengar stood out: a man caught between duty, justice, and conscience, whose internal struggle shaped much of the novel’s emotional heart.

The three Letherii brothers — Tehol, Hull, and Brys — were vivid contrasts. Hull, consumed by bitterness and revenge; Tehol, subversive and sardonic; and Brys, the King's Champion, whose quiet loyalty and moral clarity made him a surprising anchor to the story. Their differing paths created a layered portrait of what loyalty, resistance, and integrity can mean in a crumbling empire.

The narrative’s structure — told from multiple perspectives including Trull, Rhulad, Tehol (with Bugg), and Brys, along with key voices like Udinaas and Seren Pedac — was classic Erikson. Each arc ends on a cliffhanger, and each perspective deepens the thematic complexity. The first third of the book took time to build, but once the emotional connections settled in, the pacing became relentless. I was turning pages as much out of concern for the characters as for plot resolution.

The world of Lether and the Edur felt narrower and more insular than the grand scope of the first four Malazan books. But this felt intentional — a portrait of two civilizations locked into self-delusion and isolation, unable to see the wider consequences of their obsessions with commerce, conquest, and prophecy.

I was especially struck by Erikson’s critique of unbridled capitalism, rigid loyalty, and the hunger for power as an end in itself. Rhulad’s fate is one of the most tragic I’ve encountered in fantasy — a horror not of blood, but of being devoured from within by the very forces that promised salvation.

Midnight Tides may have begun as the outlier in the series, but by the end, it earned its place — strange, darkly comic, and emotionally searing.
