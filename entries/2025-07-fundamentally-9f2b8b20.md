#bookclub

This short novel orbits a tight cast of characters, some of whom felt exaggerated to the point of caricature. Lionie (Nadia’s boss) and Tom (the macho security guy) especially seemed sketched to make a satirical point rather than emerge as fully formed individuals.

Much of the humor relies on cultural and tonal dissonance: Sara’s brash East London persona inserted into a Baghdad aid mission, and Nadia, a lapsed Muslim, mocking her heritage with secular detachment. I often laughed — but couldn’t always tell if I was laughing with these women or at them. Is the novel offering solidarity through irony, or critique masquerading as comedy?

The highlight for me was Nadia’s journey toward self-awareness. Her realization that she’s neglected others while clinging to Sara out of selfish need was honest and affecting — it gave emotional heft to a story that could otherwise veer into satire alone.

But I kept returning to the book’s portrayal of Iraq itself. The country and its people felt underdeveloped — more like a setting than a presence. Given Younis’ background as an aid worker in Iraq, I expected more complexity. Is the shallow depiction a reflection of Nadia’s limited worldview, blinkered by her UN bubble? Or is it a sign of the author’s own disengagement from the very world she’s writing about?

The sympathetic Iraqi driver/security guard, Farris, is drawn with warmth — but even he feels slightly idealized, more emblematic than individual. His presence hints at the richness of Iraqi life beyond the UN compound, yet he remains at the story’s margins. That left me wondering: is this narrative distance a deliberate reflection of Nadia’s limited viewpoint, or does it reveal a blind spot in the novel itself?

Fundamentally left me more uncertain than satisfied — but perhaps that’s part of the point. It opens up important questions about identity, satire, and complicity that linger well beyond the final page.
