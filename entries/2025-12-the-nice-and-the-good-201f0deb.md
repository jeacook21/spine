I read the introduction to _The nice and the Good_ by Catherine Bates. I wish I had not. The introduction colored my own interpretation of the novel and I cant escape the fact that Bates has influences, poluted, my own reading. With that given as a caveat here is my reflection on the _The nice and the good_.

First it is a beautiful Novel. The atmospheres are beauifully drawn. The worlds of Dorset, Domestic, Demonic and Professional London are all ddistinct in feel and impression. Murdoch draws pictures in the mind with excellent use of metaphor and emotion. For example:

> The summer melancholy of suburban London, gritty, contingent, trivial, hung over the scene like an old familiar smell.

- Chapter Seventeen, page 142

Thematically the novel graples with what is good and what is bad but also what is moral and unmoral. Goodness without forgiveness or love is shown as empty or "nice". Characters are moral ideas but also human. Octavian and Kate are shallow hedonistic creatures concerned more with how they are loved by others and their own centrality to their lives.

Kate:
> "you are all my dear -- Children"
Duncane:
> "Slaves"

- Chapter Thirty, page 267

> "Her people seemed preocuiped, almost too preocuipied to rejoice as they ought to at her reapearence"

- Chapter thirty, page 268

This is in contrast to Mary, Willy, Paula and of course Ducane. Octavian and Kate's love is narcississtic as contrast to the love that Mary, Paula, Pierce and Barbie all demonstrate.

Does the novel achieve its purpose? Yes. The central thesis is that there is no greater evel than man's inhumanity to man and that no good can come except through love is manifest deeply in each deeply drawn relationship. Ducane with Jessica, his obsession with Kate and ultimately his love and admiration for Mary.
As Kate says _"Loving people matters doesnt it? Really nothing else matters except that".

And the world of Dorset in its comfortable boeorgeous superiority can be seen in contrast with Radeechy's and McGrath.

> "How natural it is to cure the pains of vice by positive develry. Vice itself is a rescue from the misery of guilt"

There are three problems I have with the novel. Its treatment of Homosexuality through Uncle Theo, its portrail of class and the ending.

Theo is a homosexual and pederast:

> "Picturing himself as taking the naked Kouros (Pierce) into his arms"

and is portraied not as a "real" man:

> "it occured to no one to count Uncle Theo as a man"

In addition there is the episode in Idia where there was a scandal between Theo and a Novice Monk which ended in death. I dont' see the purpose of Theo in the plot or indeed in the moral lesson except perhaps to show homosexuality as a vice - lust without love - perhaps. Where jessica is active in her lust for love perhaps Theo is a contrast to provide the fullness of the lesson? I felt this was not neccessary and I resent this portrait. It could be argued that Theo is a moral counterpoint to Ducane - where Theo's desire is intelectual and controling could be a warning to the fate of Ducane - especially in relations to Jessica. But still the Pederastic nature of Theo's desire was uneccessary to draw this portrait.

Class and the privalege of the english upper middle cannot be ingonred in her group. I found the treatment of those of a lower station than the Dorset set as problematic. McGrath, Judy and Fives are by turns; blackmailers, sexually unfaithful (if not a sex worker) and unreliable theives. The middle class characters are portraied with complexity, able to be both good and bad. It is hard to find good in McGrath. It confuses me why at the end Ducane gives McGrath a job as his servant and driver. Is this to show the moral superiority of Ducane in his abilty to forgive and proviee a second chance? This doesn't make sense to me.

The happy ending between Mary and John Ducane felt sudden and unexpected. One could argue that the realisation of love, when confused by circumstance, pride and missplaced assumptions, is sudden but i felt there were not enough breadcrumbs laid by Murdoch to make this seem, if not inevitable, but possible. A lesson on pain, patience, friendship and love could have better been drawn by deepening the interaction between John and Mary. Rather I'm left with the impression that love sprang after Ducane heroically saved Pierce (Mary's son). This feels a shallow reason to fall in love.

This sudden ending is why I gave four stars rather than five. The ending between Mary & John felt abrupt. But i enjoyed and admired _The nice and the good_ and will look to read more Murdoch in future.
