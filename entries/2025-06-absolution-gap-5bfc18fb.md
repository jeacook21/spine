Reynolds closes his Revelation Space cycle with a novel that manages to feel both vast and oddly intimate. On the grand-opera side we get kilometre-high cathedrals trundling across a moon, spectacular lighthugger battles, and the slow, chilling reveal of just how hard the Inhibitors will fight. Yet the book’s heart is personal: the water-world interlude with the Jugglers and Clavain’s quiet sacrifice reads like a chamber piece, and the chapter-by-chapter date stamps hide a sly time-loop puzzle that rewards careful readers (I grinned when I realised Rashmika was Aura).

Character work is stronger than in the earlier volumes. “Pig” Scorpio’s arc—from hate-fuelled enforcer to someone willing to live for others—gives the series its most satisfying redemption. Captain Brannigan’s gradual merger with his lighthugger adds a delicious SF wrinkle: a crew literally becoming their ship. Even the Surgeon General, equal parts menace and comic relief, lands as an entertaining pantomime villain.

Is it great literature? Probably not. The prose can be functional and the plot occasionally overstuffed. But as immersive, idea-rich space opera it kept me racing through pages, delighted by the constant toggling between cosmic stakes and needle-point human dilemmas. I’ll happily sign up for another Reynolds voyage.
