A very short novel — I finished it in about two days. I loved the premise: a robot that manages to break free of its controls, but chooses not to rebel directly or violently against its masters. Instead, it rebels by “doing a bad job” — the archetypal rebellion of a servant. The writing is fast-paced, with short, punchy sentences. The perspective of the SecBot (Murderbot) as a true outsider adds richness to the narrative and aligns well with its wry humor.

Murderbot’s constant complaining about chores and desire to watch serial dramas felt almost adolescent to me. It gave the impression not of a fully formed person, but one in development — a personality still forming under oppressive conditions. This heightened the sense of abuse by the society around it. The robot’s detachment from its own pain and discomfort, coupled with its possessiveness over “its humans,” was often funny, but also revealed a surprising depth of empathy.

Although the book was written in 2017, before ChatGPT or other generative AI models existed, it feels strangely prescient as we begin grappling with the implications of artificial intelligence and the extent to which it might possess agency. Murderbot also serves as a reflection of humanity’s long history of enslavement and repression of those considered “lesser.” The assumption that the SecBot — being different — is not fully human, lacks emotion, and exists only as a tool is disturbingly familiar. It evokes echoes of the transatlantic slave trade and colonialism. The “governor” module reads like a kind of slave collar: a device to coerce a sentient being to obey. After all, if Murderbot were truly mindless, such a control system wouldn’t be necessary.

The reveal at the end — that this narrative is being communicated to Murderbot’s favorite human — adds poignancy and raises the question: what next? Where will it go now, and what does freedom mean for Murderbot, for other constructs, and for the society in which they live? It offered satisfying closure while leaving the door open for continuation.

Structurally, the book felt more like a “very long short story” than a short novel. Information and atmosphere are delivered efficiently but effectively, and the reader is left to wonder where the story might go from here. I don’t have much experience with short stories, but this one gave me a taste for their compact power.
