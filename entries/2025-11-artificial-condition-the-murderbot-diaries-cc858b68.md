> An entertaining action sci-fi novel with a satirical edge and a deeper philosophical question: what does it mean to be "Human"

The second installment of the Murderbot Diaries was highly entertaining if a little shallow as more interesting threads such as the motivations of the SexBot, the role and rights of artificial intelligence are not explored. The job Murderbot takes to reach his old mining camp — to uncover what happened and confront his guilt — felt less engaging than his developing relationship with ART the mind of the research come transport vessel he hitches a ride with.

ART and Murderbot's relationship is the strongest element of the novel. ART feels like both little brother and concerned parent. He is both passive and powerful, capable of astonishing feats yet lacking Murderbot’s physical agency. ART's body is a ship and unable to interact with the universe at a human level. His childlike fear of scary stories made him feel human though an emerging and embryonic personality. Through his interactions and collaboration with Murderbot ART grows. He no longer takes a passive role and starts to act.

Murderbot explores his motivations and behaviors much as someone trying to work through their demons and the source of their bad choices and behavior. He is trying to grow and improve himself and realises that he no longer has to take orders. What is interesting here is his concern and care for his charges. Is it a result of his programming, a matter of pride, a left-over from the human elements of his cyborg body? We and he are left guessing.

Wells focuses on the action and lets the philosophical tenets of her story telling seep out through the cracks. You come to a Murderbot novel for the humor, the action and the satire on a post-democratic corporate world where life is cheap and money is all that matters.  Wells doesn't patronize her readers. She provides an entertaining tale with a little philosophical spice. Her moral point of view is both implicit (the interactions between characters) and explicit (the role of SexBots, SecBots, bonds and contracts). She paints a future no one (certainly not me) would want to inhabit — an extrapolation of the hypercapitalist and libertarian impulses of Wells’s native US.

I found parallels with Margaret Atwood's MaddAddam universe where technology, human nature and the world are subverted by greed and corporate interests. Though Well's world is full of wry humor and feels, at least to me, lighter than the worlds that Atwood tends to draw.

While my criticism of this specific novel as "shallow", are justified this is only entry 2 in a series of novels. There is plenty of time for the themes of free will, motivation and deeper characterisation of both Murderbot and other (perhaps returning) characters to deepen.
