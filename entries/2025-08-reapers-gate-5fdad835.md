I was captivated by the adventure and again found myself enthralled by the characters and the world Erikson has woven. This novel felt like a set piece — packed with major events, considerable plot development, and the resolution of a three-book arc with Rhulad’s tragic existence finally brought to an end.

As the seventh of ten, it reads like the moment when the Crippled God is knocked back, though clearly he will resurge dangerously — so many paths remain open. The morality of the story is again clear: revenge leads only to despair, love endures as a force that cannot be defeated, and unbridled capitalism remains inherently exploitative.

The emotional currents are powerful. The arc of the Sengar brothers is particularly poignant: Fear dies without fulfilling his quest, disillusioned with Seren Pedac as the focus of his purpose; Trull finds love but dies before he can build a new life; and Rhulad’s vanity and desperate need to be perceived as strong prevent him from breaking his cycle, making him the embodiment of brute power and thoughtless violence. Alongside these tragedies, the return of Hedge and his reunion with Fiddler, as well as the tragic yet redemptive journeys of Seren Pedac and Udinaas, provided moments of deep humanity.

Erikson manages the emotional pitch with care. The horror — such as the torture inflicted by the Patriotists — is juxtaposed with humour from Hedge, Fiddler, Tehol, and Bugg, creating tonal balance without diminishing either.

His narrative style keeps the reader guessing. Each new segment begins with a description of place, leaving us momentarily unmoored before characters emerge. This creates a slow reveal, a focusing-in, that mirrors the way we piece together his vast world.

Reaper’s Gale stands as one of Erikson’s most morally complex and emotionally resonant works so far.
