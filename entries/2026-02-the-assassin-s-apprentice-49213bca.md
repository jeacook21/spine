The Assasin's Apprentice enthralled me. I reluctantly put it asside when I had to sleep and I picked it up eagerly in the morning when I worke. The reading of it filled a rainy weekend which otherwise would have been dull and grey.

Fitz's world is beautifully drawn. The boyish wonder and longing for friendship, love and beloying kept me turning page after page. The world of the Six Dutchies feels familar yet alien. Hobb pains an idealised picture of a medievel court with an Arthurian flavor. Magic and myth is there but it is subtle, woven into the fabric of world just as a bard would tell a tail of Merlin round a fire.

Characters a richly drawn, each with their own idosynracies, voice and smell. The realms of taste and smell are used to great effect by Hobbs to build our understanding of the world. The smell of sweat, leather, horses, dogs - even shit and piss give the Six Dutchies a vitality that is misisng in other works of Fantacy. The Six Dutchis feels alive.

The pacing is excelent. The sotry carried from early, and tragic, childhood to a teenage asasin keeps the reader intrigues. The distant threat of the red-ships and the intimate danger of King Shrewd's court with the very real and horrific reality of the "Forged" (a truly diabolical invenstion by Hobb) keep the tension high.

Morally this is also building to be an interesting tale. When is it right and wrong to kill, when do you think for yourself and when do you abdicate responsibility to those above you and follow orders? These were posed lightly in this first novel, I hope they are deepened in subsequent volumes.

A rich world of complex characters and self discovery. Leaving me with an anticipation for what comes next.
