> A new, grittier, Culture series?

_Embers of War_ was a short and enjoyable romp through space. The pacing was fast, and the action felt natural rather than contrived. Powell wears his influences openly: Iain M. Banks’s _Culture_ novels, the extended _Star Wars_ universe, and perhaps _The Expanse_ in its gritty portrayal of competing human factions.

I liked Powell’s use of multiple first-person perspectives to build a more rounded view of events. When the story unfolds aboard the Trouble Dog, this structure works well. The shifting viewpoints of the three main protagonists often overlap or contradict one another, giving the reader a layered understanding of what is happening. In contrast, when a character is isolated—on the run, in exile, or alone on a distant posting—the same technique limits what we can see. Yet this narrowing of perspective mirrors the characters’ isolation, reinforcing their sense of confinement and loneliness.

Where I felt the novel faltered was in its treatment of Nod. Clearly a sophisticated and intelligent being, his limited English and terse inner monologue (“Eat, Serve, Sleep, Return to Mother Tree”) give the impression of simplicity rather than difference. This felt like a missed opportunity. It risks implying that a being who cannot communicate in human terms must also think in shallow ways. Science fiction has often reflected our tendency to confuse linguistic limitation with lack of intelligence. Powell could have explored the contrast between how the crew perceive Nod and how he actually experiences the world. That might have deepened the novel’s themes of empathy and understanding across species.

The foreshadowing of the Marble Fleet’s mysterious “Enemy” adds depth and gives the story room to grow into a series. The glimpses of strange creatures lurking in extra-dimensional space hint at a wider, darker universe. This is a trope I tend to enjoy—the unknowable threat that waits beyond perception. It reminded me of the monsters in Tchaikovsky’s _Final Architecture_ series, or the “demons” and “ultimates” in Christopher Ruocchio’s _Sun Eater_ books, and even the horror of _Event Horizon_. Powell’s treatment of this idea left enough mystery to intrigue without resorting to a cliffhanger. It will be interesting to see how he develops it in later volumes.

_Embers of War_ may not reach the moral and philosophical heights of the _Culture_ series, but it captures something of its spirit: a fascination with conscience, choice, and the possibility of redemption in the vast, cold arena of space.
