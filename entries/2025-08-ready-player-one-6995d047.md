The book’s three-part structure worked well, each section mirroring the quest stages within the OASIS and Wade’s experiences in the real world.

- **Part 1 builds the world**, context, and Wade/Parzival’s backstory. In both the OASIS and real life, this is about finding his footing — completing the first quest stage while establishing himself as a “gunter.”

- **Part 2 sees the pace pick up**. In the OASIS, Wade completes two more quest stages; in reality, he faces a direct confrontation with IOI and deepens his relationships with the other “Top 5.”

- **Part 3 mirrors a quest of three final stages**: his romance with Art3mis, the move to Columbus, and his infiltration of IOI as an indentured worker.

I was struck by the similarities with two 20th-century classics, suggesting Ready Player One may have been influenced by them:

1. 1984 — IOI’s power and surveillance echo the Party’s control, and the poverty and hopelessness of everyday life parallel that of the proles. Wade’s time as an indentured worker strongly recalls Winston Smith’s situation. The difference is in tone: Wade wins, where Winston is destroyed.

2. The Catcher in the Rye — Wade’s outsider perspective, social isolation, and confusion with the world mirror Holden’s alienation and critical eye.

The pacing was excellent, reading as much like a thriller as science fiction. The critique of late-stage capitalism was clear, especially in its portrayal of the exploitation of the indentured and the casual disregard for “ordinary” people.

##### Reflection

Ready Player One was far more than just a nostalgia-fuelled adventure. It blended the energy of a treasure hunt with sharp social commentary, weaving in themes of isolation, surveillance, and systemic injustice without losing its momentum. I came away impressed by how well it balanced excitement with depth.
