#bookclub

This was one of the most immersive and thought-provoking novels I’ve read for book club. At first, I was struck by the contradictions: glorious descriptions of food set against social judgment, self-judgment, and the suffocating weight of gender roles. Yuzuki writes entirely from a woman’s perspective, and the novel becomes a feminist lens — critical of patriarchal hypocrisy, yet deeply attentive to women’s inner contradictions.

Food emerges as more than sensual detail; it is a metaphor for power, freedom, and self-expression. When Rika feels strength “with each movement of her jaw,” I began to see how appetite itself could be resistance. Her changing body unsettles others not because it harms them, but because it defies cultural norms. This rejection of obedience makes her body a site of threat — a reminder that living outside expectation destabilises the social order.

Yet the novel also reveals the other side of patriarchy. Men, too, are trapped in impossible standards: strength equated with silence, shame equated with failure. Rita’s father withdraws from his family out of misplaced shame, destroying relationships that could have endured if he had trusted their love. Makoto, too, seems imprisoned — as though men and women occupy separate locked rooms, able to glimpse each other only through smoked glass.

I found this poignant rather than judgmental. The tragedy lies not in individual weakness, but in the fear of vulnerability — the opportunities for connection that collapse under social constraint. At times, reading left me feeling sad and claustrophobic, recalling my own memories of being closeted as a teenager and desperately trying to conform. But this was clarifying rather than alienating: it helped me connect my own experience of social expectation to a feminist perspective. I do not live by myself. Literature at its best does this — it allows you to see and feel the world through someone else’s eyes.

What I admired most was that Yuzuki resists the fairy-tale neatness of something like Lessons in Chemistry. Rika is no perfect heroine, but contradictory and real. This gave me not only intellectual understanding but genuine empathy. By the end, the tone was hopeful: Rika begins to live by her own standards, finding meaning in friendship, love, and self-reliance. Even her older male friend finds fulfilment when he begins to live authentically, suggesting that both women and men can escape the cages of expectation.

Butter left me with the sense that hope is fragile but possible — that liberation comes not from grand gestures, but from daring to live your own life, and welcoming support from those who love you for who you are.
